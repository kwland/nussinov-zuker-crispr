06

Learn

The whole idea,
in plain steps

No background needed. Seven short steps, start to finish.

1

What is RNA?

RNA is a long strand made of just four letters: A, C, G and U.

It does not stay in a straight line. It bends and sticks to itself, making shapes.

2

How does it fold?

Certain letters stick together. A goes with U, and G goes with C. G can also stick to U, but more weakly.

When letters that are far apart on the strand stick together, they pull it into loops and stems. That shape is what we predict.

3

Two ways to guess the shape

A strand could fold many ways. We wrote two programs that each pick a best fold, using different rules:

  • Nussinov: the fold with the most sticky pairs.
  • Zuker: the most stable fold, the one that holds together best.
4

Why bother writing two?

If we just called a program that already exists, matching it would prove nothing.

Our two work in completely different ways. So when they land on the same pairs, that agreement is real evidence the shape is right. We also test them against shapes scientists already solved, as an answer key.

5

What has this got to do with CRISPR?

CRISPR uses a short piece of RNA, called a guide, to find one exact spot in DNA.

The guide has to be free to stick to that DNA. The tip that touches first is called the seed.

If the guide folds up and hides its own seed, it cannot grab the DNA well, so it works worse. Predicting the fold lets us spot the weak guides early.

6

Is this AlphaFold?

No, and the difference is worth knowing.

AlphaFold predicts the full 3D shape of proteins with a huge trained model. That is a hard problem that needs serious computers and takes minutes.

We predict secondary structure: only which letters pair with which. That is a solved problem with fast, exact algorithms from the 1980s, which is why our folds finish in a few milliseconds. The 3D view here just arranges that flat answer in space so you can look at it.

7

07

How it works

Two ways to fold
the same molecule

Both are dynamic programming: solve every short piece, remember the answer, then build the bigger answer out of the smaller ones. They differ only in what counts as a good answer.

Model 1

Nussinov, 1978

Goal: the most base pairs

N[i,j] = max N[i+1, j] N[i, j-1] N[i+1, j-1] + pair(i,j) maxk N[i,k] + N[k+1,j]
  • Every allowed pair is worth exactly +1
  • Runs in n³ time and is easy to trace by hand
  • Ignores physics, so a stack and a lone pair count the same
  • Over pairs in practice, inventing pairs that would not hold
Model 2

Zuker, 1981

Goal: the lowest free energy

V[i,j] = min eH(i,j) hairpin eS(i,j) + V[i+1,j-1] stack eL(i,j,k,l) + V[k,l] bulge / internal WM[i+1,k] + WM[k+1,j-1] + a multiloop
  • Stacking holds the strand together, loops cost energy
  • Uses nearest neighbour energies measured in a lab
  • Lone pairs get penalised away on their own
  • This is the idea behind ViennaRNA and RNAfold

Energy model

What actually holds RNA together

This is Zuker's stabilising term. Each number is the energy of stacking one pair straight onto another, in kcal/mol at 37 °C. More negative means more stable. G and C rich stacks are the strongest, which is why G/C content predicts guide behaviour at all.

These ten values are the published Turner and Xia nearest neighbour parameters. Our G·U wobble stacks, loop tables and multiloop term are simplified on purpose, and they are documented as such in the code. So treat our kcal/mol as a ranking, not a lab measurement. ViennaRNA is the real reference, and chapter 05 shows how close we get to it.

What this project shows, and what it does not

Holds up

  • Both recurrences are written from scratch. On GGGAAACCC our Zuker returns −1.12 kcal/mol, which matches the number you get deriving it by hand from the stacking table.
  • On the three answer key shapes, our Zuker scores a perfect F1 of 1.00, including the tRNA cloverleaf. Our Nussinov gets the cloverleaf badly wrong, which is exactly the point.
  • Against ViennaRNA on 30 real guides, our energies correlate at about r = 0.87. Different method, same answer.

Does not

  • Seed openness does not predict editing efficiency on the full dataset (Spearman ≈ 0.01). We went looking for that link and it is not there. Chapter 04 has the plot.
  • Our kcal/mol will not match ViennaRNA exactly, because our loop and wobble terms are simplified. Our energies run consistently less negative.
  • Real editing also depends on chromatin, repair, off target effects and Cas9 itself. None of that is here.
